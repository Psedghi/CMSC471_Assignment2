Gender and Education: Two Ways to Tell the Story

CMSC471 Assignment 2 | Elizabeth Ipe & Parsa Sedghi | Data: Gender Equality Indicators 1960–2017

Education access is shaped by structural barriers(like poverty, geography, and fertility rates) that affect how far children progress through the educational pipeline. The white-hat visualization looks at secondary school enrollment globally, where those structural forces are most visible: high-fertility countries consistently show lower and often negative gender gaps, meaning boys outpace girls. The black-hat visualization shifts focus to university (tertiary) enrollment in a hand-picked set of wealthy Western nations, cherry-picking the slice of the pipeline where women have surged ahead and framing it as a male crisis.

Definitions to know:

Secondary education refers to middle and high school (roughly ages 12–18).

Tertiary education refers to university and college programs beyond secondary school.

Write-up

Dataset

We used the World Bank Gender Statistics dataset, which provides data between 1970–2019, to model different levels of the education pipeline. For our white-hat visualization, we looked at School enrollment, secondary (female/male % gross) and Fertility rate, total (births per woman) to show how structural barriers shape access at the middle/high school level over time. In the black-hat chart, we used School enrollment, tertiary, female (% gross) and School enrollment, tertiary, male (% gross) for six hand-picked high-income countries. One thing to note about the data is that gross enrollment rates can go over 100% when students outside the standard school-age range are included.

Visual One: White Hat

We used a scatter plot with fertility rate on the x-axis and gender gap in secondary school enrollment (female − male, percentage points) across 186 countries. A positive value on the y-axis indicates that more girls are enrolled than boys. There is background shading on the graph, where blue means more girls are enrolled and orange means that more boys are enrolled. Each country is a point, and each point is colored according to three different categories: blue for high (>90%), green for mid (60–90%), and orange-red for low (<60%). The user is also able to choose the year they want to view underneath the graph using the slider. We also included a legend with information about the groupings and the y-axis to help the user understand the graph. Overall, we can see that countries with higher fertility rates tend to have lower or negative enrollment gaps, while countries with lower fertility rates tend to have higher enrollment in girls. This design is completely transparent, meaning that no countries were selected specifically to prove a point.

Visual Two: Black Hat

This visualization is based on the same dataset but is purposely deceptive. We chose only six high-income countries — United States, Canada, Sweden, Iceland, Norway, and the United Kingdom — and showed that they have higher female enrollment rates to make the user assume that there is a gap between women and men worldwide. We purposely hid the fact that the gap between male and female enrollment worldwide is only about 6 percentage points. We also left out the fact that men enroll equally or at higher rates in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. Additionally, we started the y-axis at 40% instead of 0% so that there is a more dramatic visual difference. For example, the Iceland difference appears very large even though both rates represent high levels of access globally. Finally, we created a biased message in the title and caption even though men’s enrollment has also been increasing steadily (women’s enrollment has simply increased faster). Together, these aspects portray a misleading narrative that men are getting pushed aside.
